Gaps in WCAG and Suggested Improvements

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) set important standards but they often overlook the needs of older adults, especially around memory, cognitive load, and adaptability. Many websites are built by younger developers with vastly different digital experiences. 75% of adults 65+ use the internet (Pew Research), but they still face tech barriers. As Laura Carstensen, Director of the Stanford Center on Longevity, notes, “The challenge is converting a world built by and for the young into a world that supports... a population that lives 100 years and beyond.” Accessible design and inclusive design guidelines aren't explicitly designing with age in mind.

What WCAG Gets Right

  • 1.2.2 / 1.2.4 – Captions for hearing loss

  • 1.4.3 / 1.4.4 – Contrast and text resizing

  • 2.2.1 / 2.2.2 – Adjustable time limits

  • 2.5.5 – Large clickable areas

But these don't address cognitive changes, memory retention, or adaptability.

Where WCAG Falls Short

Current web design assumes generalizations but aging is personal, gradual, and non-linear.

“Design heuristics and accessibility guidelines may not cover Elders.” – IBM Accessible Design for Aging Populations

  • Focus is mostly on sensory/physical impairments, not memory or cognitive aging.

  • Lack of support for auto-save, progress tracking, or session continuity.

  • Navigation isn't recall-friendly. It's often lacking cues, reminders, or clear progress indicators.

The Risk Ageism in Design

When older adults are left out of design decisions, the result is often unintentional ageism:

  • Tiny click targets hard for those with arthritis or tremors

  • Low contrast that strains aging eyes

  • Timed logouts or error-prone forms with no chance to recover work

Older users shouldn’t have to “catch up” to design. They should be included from the start.

WCAG Best Practices Suggestions

WCAG could go further by encouraging these improvements and best practices:

  • Persistent login pages and “Continue where you left off”

  • Step-by-step guides and simplified navigation

  • Auto-complete / predictive text to reduce typing burden

  • Timeout warnings and form data recovery

  • Avoiding assumptions in interface and language

  • Settings to easily reconfigure GUI for larger text and simpler navigation

Aging users aren’t a minority but a growing part of the digital world. Making the web accessible for them means rethinking what “accessibility” really includes. Designing with age in mind, not as an afterthought, sets users up for success in navigating the growing digital landscape.
