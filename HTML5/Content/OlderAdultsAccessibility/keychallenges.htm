Accessibility Barriers for Older Users

Many users find it intuitive to navigate websites. Locating the home button, search bar, or menu feels like second nature. However, this is not a universal experience. Older users, specifically those unfamiliar with digital technology or experiencing age-related impairments, may find website navigation overwhelming and unattainable.

Age-Related Accessibility Barriers

As people age, they experience natural changes that impact their ability to use digital interfaces. These changes create barriers to web accessibility that must be addressed.

Vision and Hearing Decline:

  • Conditions like cataracts and age-related macular degeneration make reading from screens difficult. These impairments reduce the ability to interpret visual fields and web page contrast as “Eyes become less sensitive and less able to detect light, colors, and detail over time” (Rodrigues et al).

  • Age-related hearing loss begins in middle adulthood. 20% of people aged 45–54 experience hearing difficulties. That number increases to 75% by age 75. This causes challenges in watching online help tutorials or joining video calls.

Motor Skills and Cognitive Changes

  • Users also experience physical navigation challenges from a decline in motor skills due to reduced strength, speed, and hand tremors. These impairments results in difficulty using a mouse, keyboard, or touchscreen device.

  • Cognitive impairments such as reduced memory, slower perception speed, and lower attention span cause difficulty in comprehending text and multitasking. A 2020 IRJAES study noted memory recall problems and confusion navigating websites.

  • ACM (2018) research found: Difficulty distinguishing links from plain text and struggles with searching for and locating information.

     

    Why This Matters

    The 2000 U.S. Census Bureau report estimates that there are currently thirty-five million Americans aged sixty-five and over. Yet older generations are often not the intended audience when websites are created. They are pushed to the side, left to navigate a digital world not built with them in mind, all while managing the consequences of age-related impairments.

    As Holsapple notes, “System developers have hitherto focused, perhaps unintentionally, on the research and development of interface designs for the young and the healthy, although research concerning designs for the older and less able sections of society is emerging” (Holsapple et al). Older adults who may be immobile or living in an assisted living community are often left without the tools needed to communicate easily with the outside world, further deepening the need to create an accessible digital place for all.

    Accessibility for older users is not just about convenience. It’s about making space for them to stay connected, independent, and included in a rapidly digital world.
